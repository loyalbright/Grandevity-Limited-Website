Some songs do not ask us to leave the city.

They simply preserve a quiet light amid the traffic, unread messages, crowded streets and countless illuminated windows.

**Mountain in the City Lights** (《人境見山》) is a Mandarin male-vocal R&B song inspired by Tao Yuanming’s celebrated poem *Drinking Wine, No. 5*. It begins among an Eastern Jin cottage, chrysanthemums beneath the eastern hedge and the distant Southern Mountain, before gradually entering the emotional landscape of modern urban life.

The song does not portray Tao Yuanming merely as a recluse who withdrew from society. Nor does it interpret retreat as an escape from reality.

Instead, it asks a question that remains deeply relevant today:

**While still living in the human world, can we find a mountain within ourselves?**

### From “Dwelling in the Human World” to a Tide of City Lights

Tao Yuanming’s *Drinking Wine, No. 5* has endured for centuries not simply because it depicts a tranquil pastoral scene, but because it poses a question that still speaks directly to modern life:

> I built my dwelling in the human world,  
> Yet hear no clamour of carriage or horse.  
> You ask how this can be?  
> When the heart is distant, the place becomes remote.

Tao Yuanming did not completely abandon the “human world.”

He still lived among other people and remained part of earthly life. What changed was his relationship with its noise. He no longer allowed the clamour outside him to determine the direction within.

The creative journey of *Mountain in the City Lights* begins with that idea.

The human world, carriages, eastern hedge, Southern Mountain and returning birds of the original poem first become the autumn mist, abandoned steps, evening hills and flowing stream encountered by a later traveller:

> A later traveller seeks the ancient path,  
> His solitary steps entering the autumn mist.  
> How could the traces ever have been far away?  
> When the dust within grows still, the road opens wide.

Centuries later, another traveller follows the spiritual path left by Tao Yuanming and walks once more into the autumn mountains.

He is not searching for a literal cottage or a forgotten trail that can be located on a map. He is searching for something more difficult to find: a way to settle the heart after years of striving.

Then an evening breeze rises from the old mountain, crosses the centuries and enters the lights of the modern city.

### Not Leaving the City, but Learning to Be Still Within It

The song fully enters the present day when the chorus begins:

> Amid the human tide and city lights, I hear old dreams grow wide;  
> I leave the unanswered messages for the night to slow.

The carriage noise of the ancient world has become the notifications, deadlines, traffic and constant demands of contemporary life.

We are expected to reply immediately, remain constantly available and continually prove that we are still moving forward.

*Mountain in the City Lights* offers another possibility.

Some messages can wait.

Some answers do not need to be found tonight.

And on certain evenings, we may choose not to answer the world immediately. We may first give ourselves the time to become whole again.

The song does not reject the city or romanticise a life of seclusion. It suggests that genuine stillness does not necessarily wait in some distant landscape.

It may emerge in the very heart of the crowd.

### Urban R&B: Bringing an Ancient “True Meaning” into a Modern Heartbeat

*Mountain in the City Lights* draws upon the warmth and emotional restraint of early-2000s Mandopop R&B.

A reflective male vocal, mid-tempo shuffled rhythm, Fender Rhodes electric piano, nylon-string guitar, warm neo-soul bass, smooth backing harmonies and subtle guqin and bamboo flute accents create the atmosphere of a late-night city gradually becoming still.

This is not a traditional “ancient-style” arrangement. It is a contemporary Mandarin R&B song with the spirit of classical Chinese poetry beneath its surface.

*   **Rhodes piano** glows like light through a city window: warm, intimate and slightly distant.
*   **Neo-soul bass and R&B drums** carry the pace, pressure and heartbeat of modern life.
*   **Guqin and bamboo flute** are not decorative symbols of the East, but echoes of an evening breeze travelling across time.
*   **Male vocal** avoids grand declarations, allowing reflection and release to unfold through restraint.

The ancient and modern worlds finally meet in one of the song’s defining lines:

> **Though the whole city surges, it cannot disturb the slightest ripple within.**

The streets remain loud. Life’s problems have not disappeared.

Yet true peace does not begin when the world finally falls silent. It begins when the world can no longer so easily disturb the heart.

---

### “Leave One Light of Silence to Reveal the Southern Mountain”

The song ultimately arrives at these lines:

> After half a lifetime of striving, I learn which worldly thoughts can be erased;  
> **I leave one light of silence to reveal the Southern Mountain.**

“Erase” is a distinctly modern verb.

Every day, we delete messages, photographs, documents and things we no longer need. But we rarely ask whether the burdens accumulated within us—comparison, anxiety, resentment and attachment—can also be removed.

The “Southern Mountain” is therefore no longer only the mountain Tao Yuanming saw before him.

It can be an hour in which no one demands anything from us. It can be the freedom of no longer needing to prove ourselves. It can be the inner order we recover after half a lifetime of pursuing what once seemed indispensable.

**The mountain need not be far away. When the heart becomes still, it may appear within the human world itself.**

### The MV: From an Eastern Jin Autumn to the Lights of the Modern City

The music video for *Mountain in the City Lights* unfolds through three interwoven periods.

The opening returns to the Eastern Jin dynasty: a humble cottage, chrysanthemums beside the eastern hedge, evening mist gathering around distant mountains, birds returning at sunset and the quiet rural world of Tao Yuanming’s poem.

The newly written classical verse follows a traveller from a later age. He enters an autumn landscape of old paths, abandoned steps, streams, mist and evening hills, seeking to understand the wisdom contained in the words “when the heart is distant, the place becomes remote.”

An evening breeze then carries the story into the modern city.

Old stairways, riverside streets, neon signs, glass towers and flowing traffic surround the present-day protagonist. He moves through the crowd while gradually recovering a rhythm that belongs to him alone.

The ancient poet, the later traveller and the modern man share a similar face and spiritual presence. This is not simply a suggestion of reincarnation. It represents the same human question being asked across different centuries:

**How can we live in the human world without being overwhelmed by it?**

In the final images, the protagonist pauses at the edge of the city. Distant mountains and thousands of illuminated windows enter the same frame.

He has not left the city.

He has simply learned how to see the mountain from within it.

### A Song for Those Who Still Live in the Human World

Most of us cannot follow Tao Yuanming by leaving official life and returning to the fields.

We still have work to complete, messages to answer, responsibilities to carry and crowded roads to cross. On certain nights, we will still find ourselves surrounded by anxiety, comparison and everything left unfinished.

Yet *Mountain in the City Lights* suggests that the modern Southern Mountain may not require a distant journey.

It may be found on a quiet street after work, beside a window lit after the rain, or in an evening when we finally stop urging ourselves to move faster.

> The waves of noise recede; ten thousand thoughts return to stillness.  
> Where the evening breeze passes, no more words are needed.

May we find stillness without abandoning the human world.

May we live among the crowd and still see the mountain within.

### About the Project

*Mountain in the City Lights* is part of Grandevity Limited’s **Classical Literature Reimagined in Music** series.

Our aim is to bring classical poetry beyond textbooks, annotations and memorisation, allowing it to enter the sounds, images and lived experiences of the present day.

Centuries ago, Tao Yuanming gathered chrysanthemums beside the eastern hedge and quietly beheld the Southern Mountain.

Today, we cross a tide of people and city lights, searching for inner stillness among unanswered messages and streets that never seem to rest.

The age has changed, and the clamour of carriage and horse has taken on a different form. Yet the mountain can still be seen.

**May you remain in the human world, leave one light of silence within, and find your own Southern Mountain.**
