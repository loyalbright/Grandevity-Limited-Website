There are moments in life when the road does not end with an answer.  
It simply leads us to water.  
To a quiet creek.  
To a small stone bridge.  
To the sound of wind moving through reeds.  
To a fishing float trembling gently on the surface.

**Nanxi Wanggui — Forgotten Return by the Southern Creek** is a modern Chinese song inspired by Wang Wei’s classic Tang Dynasty poem *Zhongnan Bieye* (*Zhongnan Retreat*).

The original poem contains one of the most beloved lines in Chinese literature:

> “Walking to where the water ends,  
> I sit and watch the clouds rise.”

This is not merely a scene of nature. It is a philosophy of release. When life reaches a point where effort can go no further, one may finally learn to sit still — and watch the world continue on its own.

### From Wang Wei’s Mountain Silence to a Modern Middle-Aged Heart

Wang Wei’s poem is often read as a work of retreat and spiritual quietness. In it, the poet has reached middle age. He has grown fond of the Way, settled near the Zhongnan Mountains, wandered alone when moved by interest, and found private joy in the beauty of the landscape.

At the end of the poem, he meets an old man in the forest. They talk and laugh together, forgetting the time to return.

*Nanxi Wanggui* extends this ancient image into a modern emotional landscape.

The song imagines a middle-aged man who leaves behind the noise of the city and comes alone to the southern creek. He does not come to achieve anything. He does not even come to catch fish. He simply sits by the water, lowers his fishing rod, and lets the evening slow him down.

There, he meets an elderly fisherman. A few simple words are exchanged. Tea smoke drifts close to the water. The evening breeze touches the fishing float. Moonlight slowly spreads across the stone bridge.

Without saying it directly, the song asks:  
**What if healing is not a grand revelation?**  
**What if it is only a quiet evening beside water?**  
**What if the heart does not need to return so quickly?**

### Selected Lyric Translation & Analysis

> **“釣不回年少，卻釣出低頭一笑”**  
> *I cannot fish back my youth, but I can still fish up a quiet smile.*

This is the emotional center of the song. Youth cannot return. Time cannot be pulled back from the water. But the ability to smile — quietly, humbly, without forcing anything — can still be found again.

The line does not speak of sorrow directly. Instead, it uses fishing as a metaphor for middle-aged acceptance. The man cannot recover the years he has lost, yet by the creek, he rediscovers a small, gentle part of himself.

### Why “Forgotten Return”?

The title *Nanxi Wanggui* means “Forgotten Return by the Southern Creek.” It echoes Wang Wei’s original ending:

> “Talking and laughing, with no thought of returning.”

In the modern lyrics, this becomes:

> **“閒語忘歸期”**  
> *A few idle words, and the time to return is forgotten.*

But the song does not state this feeling too plainly. Instead, it paints a scene:

> **“來時的腳印，被蘆花輕輕掃”**  
> *The footprints from when I came are gently swept by reed flowers.*

The path is still there. The world is still waiting. But for this one evening, the need to rush back has been softened by wind, water, tea smoke, and moonlight.

### About the Creative Concept

*Nanxi Wanggui* belongs to the **Classical Literature Reimagined in Music** project.

The project seeks to bring classical Chinese poetry into modern musical language, not by simply setting old poems to melody, but by allowing ancient emotional structures to speak again through contemporary experience.

In this song, Wang Wei’s mountain retreat becomes a modern refuge. The Zhongnan Mountains become the listener’s own “southern creek” — a place where one can pause, breathe, and briefly put down the unfinished weight of life.

The song does not present escape as weakness. **It presents stillness as a form of strength.**

### Closing Reflection

Everyone needs a southern creek.

It does not have to be far away. It does not have to be hidden in the mountains. It may simply be a quiet place in the heart where the noise of the world becomes smaller.

A bridge.  
A fishing rod.  
A cup of tea.  
An old fisherman saying, gently:  

> *Slow down. That may be enough.*

And for one evening, perhaps we do not need to hurry home.
