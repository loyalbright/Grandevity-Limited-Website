Some forms of purity do not come from never approaching the mud.

They come from growing within it without allowing it to determine what we become.

**Ai Lian Shuo — On the Love of the Lotus** is a Mandarin male-vocal work composed entirely from Zhou Dunyi’s celebrated Northern Song prose text.

The lyrics preserve 100% of the original classical Chinese. Nothing has been expanded, rewritten or replaced with modern verses.

Built upon mid-tempo Mandopop, the production combines classic R&B beats, hip-hop phrasing, restrained rock energy, acoustic guitar, guzheng, erhu, pentatonic melody and vintage vinyl texture.

Zhou Dunyi begins with the many flowers of the world.

The chrysanthemum represents the recluse who withdraws from society.

The peony represents wealth, rank and worldly splendour.

The lotus grows from mud and rises through clear water, remaining unstained and refusing to turn its beauty into seduction.

Everything ultimately gathers into one statement:

> The lotus is the gentleman among flowers.

On its surface, **On the Love of the Lotus** is an essay about flowers.

At its deepest level, it asks how a person should live within the world:

**Can we remain inwardly lucid and outwardly upright while surrounded by compromise, ambition, influence and desire—without clinging, flattering or allowing the world to define our character?**

### “Among the Flowers of Water, Land, Grasses and Trees”

Zhou Dunyi does not begin by immediately praising the lotus.

He first writes:

> Among the flowers of water, land, grasses and trees, there are exceedingly many that are worthy of love.

The sentence establishes an essential premise.

Beauty is not limited to a single form, and different people may choose different objects of admiration.

The chrysanthemum, peony and lotus then appear one after another.

They gradually become more than botanical subjects.

They represent three kinds of character, three directions in life and three different relationships between the individual and society.

The MV begins with a rain-washed pond, dawn mist, bamboo shadows and an unopened writing desk.

When Zhou Dunyi enters the garden, the audience does not first encounter an abstract philosophical argument.

It encounters a living world still carrying the breath of rain.

Many flowers may be loved.

The deeper question is why a person chooses one rather than another.

---

### Tao Yuanming and the Chrysanthemum: The Life of the Recluse

Zhou Dunyi writes:

> In the Jin dynasty, Tao Yuanming alone loved the chrysanthemum.

Through its association with Tao Yuanming, the chrysanthemum becomes a symbol of reclusion.

It blooms in autumn rather than competing with the flowers of spring. It is quiet, resilient and associated with withdrawal from political power and worldly noise.

The phrase “alone loved” is important.

This is not fashion or collective enthusiasm.

It is an independent spiritual judgement.

In the MV, the chrysanthemum does not appear as an extravagant field of colour.

It emerges within a misty vision of an autumn mountain hut, a rustic fence and a solitary figure walking into distant hills.

This withdrawal is dignified and worthy of respect.

Yet it is not the final path chosen by **On the Love of the Lotus**.

---

### Since the Tang Dynasty: The Peony and Worldly Splendour

Zhou Dunyi continues:

> Since the Tang dynasty, the people of the world have greatly loved the peony.

The peony is full, richly coloured and historically associated with prosperity, status and social prestige.

Unlike Tao Yuanming’s solitary love of the chrysanthemum, love of the peony belongs to “the people of the world.”

One is a minority’s inward choice.

The other is a collective attraction toward visible splendour.

Zhou Dunyi does not deny the peony’s beauty, nor does he reduce wealth itself to moral corruption.

He places it beside the lotus so that their different values may become visible through contrast.

In the MV, the peony appears within a mist-born, Tang-inspired courtyard filled with elegant figures, lanterns, silk curtains, ornate architecture and red and pink blossoms.

The world is beautiful, luminous and powerfully attractive.

Zhou Dunyi, however, remains beside the lotus pond, quietly separated from its splendour.

---

### “Emerging from Mud Yet Remaining Unstained”

The most famous line of the essay reads:

> I alone love the lotus because it emerges from mud yet remains unstained.

The lotus is rooted in mud.

It does not grow in a perfectly clean environment, nor does it remain pure because it has never encountered corruption.

It must rise through water and darkness before opening above the surface.

Its purity is therefore not innocence without experience.

It is the ability to pass through experience without surrendering its nature.

This also marks an important difference between the chrysanthemum and the lotus.

The recluse represented by the chrysanthemum may withdraw from society.

The gentleman represented by the lotus remains within the world.

He understands its complexity and encounters influence, ambition, interest and desire, yet refuses to abandon his moral measure.

The MV places dark sediment, clear water and a white lotus within the same visual field.

Zhou Dunyi kneels formally upon the bank and observes the flower across the water without touching or picking it.

What he sees is not a flower unrelated to the mud.

He sees a life that grows from mud without allowing the mud to define it.

---

### “Washed by Clear Ripples Yet Never Alluring”

Zhou Dunyi continues:

> Washed by clear ripples yet never alluring.

The lotus is cleansed by clear water and appears bright, pure and beautiful, but never seductive or ostentatious.

“Not alluring” does not mean the rejection of beauty.

The lotus is undeniably beautiful.

Its beauty, however, does not depend upon deliberate temptation, display or the need to secure attention.

It does not deny its radiance, yet it does not convert that radiance into a demand for possession or admiration.

This is both a restrained aesthetic principle and a form of moral self-command.

The MV uses a water-level view of white lotus blossoms and clear ripples. Morning light touches the petals without turning them into excessive spectacle.

A symbolic lotus spirit appears only as a distant figure seen from behind within the mist.

She does not face the camera and is never framed as a romantic character.

She represents purity, dignity and a spiritual ideal that cannot be possessed.

---

### “Open Within and Straight Without”

The prose next turns from the appearance of the lotus to its physical structure:

> Open within and straight without, neither creeping nor branching.

The stem is hollow and continuous within, while remaining upright on the outside.

It does not produce entangling vines or uncontrolled branches.

“Open within” suggests clarity, discernment and an inward state free from obstruction.

“Straight without” suggests visible integrity and upright conduct.

The gentleman is not someone who merely performs correctness externally.

His inner understanding and outward behaviour should correspond.

He does not climb through dependence or attachment, and he does not constantly change direction in response to advantage.

In the MV, vertical lotus stems mirror Zhou Dunyi’s upright posture.

One is botanical straightness.

The other is human straightness.

White walls, timber columns and restrained pavilion architecture reinforce this order, transforming “neither creeping nor branching” into a visible composition.

---

### “Its Fragrance Travels Farther and Becomes Purer”

Zhou Dunyi writes:

> Its fragrance travels farther and becomes purer; it stands clean and upright.

Fragrance cannot be seen, yet its presence may be felt from a distance.

Virtue operates in a similar way.

Stable character does not require constant self-promotion or exaggerated declarations of goodness.

It becomes perceptible through choices, conduct and the way a person treats others over time.

The phrase “stands clean and upright” has exceptional visual force.

The lotus does not lower itself in submission, nor does it become aggressive through pride.

It simply maintains its position—clean, elevated, quiet and self-possessed.

At this line, the song enters its principal emotional ascent.

Guzheng and erhu open into a wider melodic space, while R&B rhythm and restrained rock energy support the male vocal.

The upright lotus becomes the first fully luminous moral climax of the song.

---

### “To Be Admired from Afar, Never Profanely Handled”

Zhou Dunyi completes his description with a line of remarkable restraint:

> It may be admired from afar but must not be profanely handled.

This does not mean that beauty should be sealed away.

It establishes a boundary of respect.

A life worthy of reverence is not an object to be possessed, controlled, consumed or treated carelessly.

It may be understood and admired, but intimacy must not erase its dignity.

Throughout the MV, Zhou Dunyi never reaches out to touch the lotus.

A quiet body of water remains between the observer and the flower.

That distance is not emotional coldness.

It is reverence.

In a contemporary culture that frequently turns beauty into something immediately consumable, the phrase retains a striking modern relevance.

---

### Chrysanthemum, Peony and Lotus: Three Ways of Living

Zhou Dunyi later makes his judgement explicit:

> I call the chrysanthemum the recluse among flowers;  
> the peony, the wealthy and honoured among flowers;  
> and the lotus, the gentleman among flowers.

The chrysanthemum represents reclusion.

The peony represents wealth and social distinction.

The lotus represents the virtuous gentleman.

These are not merely three positions in a simple hierarchy of good and bad.

They are three directions of life.

One may leave the world in order to preserve independence.

One may enter splendour and pursue success, rank and recognition.

The path Zhou Dunyi identifies with the gentleman is to remain within the world without allowing the world to determine one’s essential nature.

The gentleman need not abandon society.

He may work, form relationships, accept responsibility and confront practical interests.

Yet through every change, he must preserve the measure expressed by “open within and straight without.”

This is why **On the Love of the Lotus** transcends the subject of flowers.

It describes the lotus.

But it continually asks how a human being should stand.

---

### “Who Shares This Love with Me?”

The closing movement of the essay is not simply one of praise.

Zhou Dunyi writes:

> Alas!  
> Of those who love the chrysanthemum, few have been heard of after Tao.  
> Of those who love the lotus, who shares this love with me?  
> As for love of the peony, it is fitting that there are so many.

The exclamation “Alas” turns measured argument into a profound sigh.

Few after Tao Yuanming truly understand the chrysanthemum’s reclusion.

Who, then, genuinely shares the lotus ideal with Zhou Dunyi?

Those who love the prosperity and splendour represented by the peony are naturally numerous.

The power of this passage does not lie primarily in condemning the majority.

It lies in the loneliness it reveals.

A person who refuses to follow collective desire—and refuses to replace moral principle with visible success—may receive little immediate recognition from others.

The later part of the MV enters night.

Zhou Dunyi stands safely behind the corridor railing, alone before the moonlit lotus pond. In the distance, the symbolic peony courtyard remains crowded and brilliantly illuminated; nearby, white lotus blossoms remain still upon blue water.

Splendour belongs to the many.

The gentleman’s path often begins with the decision of one person.

### Mid-Tempo Mandopop Without Rewriting the Classical Text

The original **On the Love of the Lotus** is a concise and structurally precise piece of Northern Song philosophical prose.

This musical setting does not transform it into modern lyrics.

It preserves the complete original text and reorganises the listening experience through rhythm, melody, pause and emotional contrast.

The song combines:

* **Classic R&B drums**, creating a warm and stable groove.
* **Hip-hop-influenced rhythmic phrasing**, carrying the concise movement of classical Chinese syntax.
* **Restrained rock energy**, strengthening the chorus and passages of moral judgement.
* **Acoustic guitar**, providing a clear and approachable contemporary Mandopop foundation.
* **Guzheng and erhu motifs**, answering water, lotus imagery, reflection and the emotional sigh of the closing section.
* **Vintage vinyl crackle**, adding memory, time and nostalgic texture.
* **Pentatonic melodic language**, allowing the classical text to enter a contemporary song without becoming operatic.
* **An emotionally controlled Mandarin male vocal**, preserving clarity of diction and the philosophical weight of the prose.

The production does not seek oversized historical spectacle or theatrical recitation.

Its purpose is to let Zhou Dunyi’s language remain lucid, upright and intelligible while receiving the rhythmic and emotional attraction of contemporary Mandopop.

### The MV: From Rain-Washed Dawn to the Moonlit Lotus Pond

The MV remains entirely within a Northern Song visual world.

There are no modern characters and no movement between ancient and contemporary timelines.

Its imagined setting centres on a Jiangnan academy inspired by the Lianxi tradition: lotus pond, bamboo, white walls, dark tiled roofs, wooden corridors, a stone bridge and a scholar’s study.

Time moves from rain-washed dawn through afternoon, dusk and night.

Zhou Dunyi is the only principal character who remains throughout the film.

He wears the same pale celadon-grey scholar’s robe and dark cap, with a slender figure, upright posture and restrained expression.

His ideas are not represented through exaggerated acting.

They emerge through entering the garden, observing the lotus from a distance, watching clear water, walking through the corridor, pausing on the bridge, confronting the symbolic vision of peony splendour and finally standing alone beside the moonlit pond.

Eight opening images establish the historical and philosophical background.

The central section interprets the mud, water, stem, fragrance and posture of the lotus line by line.

The extended instrumental solo moves through rain striking lotus leaves, a guzheng in the study, an erhu beside the corridor, reflections beneath the stone bridge, the back-view silhouette of the lotus spirit, dusk and lantern light.

These images guide the song from natural observation toward moral reflection.

The chrysanthemum and peony appear as symbolic visions.

The lotus spirit remains distant, with her face unseen. She is not a romantic figure, but a visual embodiment of the gentlemanly ideal.

At the end, the human figure disappears.

The pond returns to stillness.

A final thread of incense remains above the writing desk.

One white lotus rises beneath the moon, leaving the phrase “the gentleman among flowers” as the final image.

### Not Merely an Essay About a Flower

**On the Love of the Lotus** has endured for nearly a thousand years not only because the phrase “emerging from mud yet remaining unstained” is beautiful.

Its deeper achievement is the transformation of abstract moral character into a natural image that can be directly seen.

Mud is the complexity of reality.

Clear ripples are environment, experience and testing.

Being open within and straight without is consistency between inner understanding and outward conduct.

Neither creeping nor branching means refusing dependence and opportunistic attachment.

Fragrance becoming purer at a distance is virtue that does not require noise.

Standing clean and upright is dignity maintained within the world.

The prohibition against profane handling establishes a spiritual boundary that must not be violated.

Zhou Dunyi does not demand that everyone abandon society.

The ideal he proposes is more difficult:

To remain within the world without being stained by it.

To recognise splendour without making splendour the only measure of life.

To understand human interest and practical reality without abandoning inward clarity and outward integrity.

The lotus remains a lotus.

Yet within language, music and image, it also becomes the reflection of every person who still wishes to preserve an uncorrupted inner life.

### About the Project

**Ai Lian Shuo — On the Love of the Lotus** is part of Grandevity Studio’s *Classical Literature Reimagined in Music* series.

The project seeks to bring classical Chinese prose beyond textbooks, annotations, recitation and memorisation, allowing it to re-enter contemporary melody, rhythm, sound, imagery and emotional life.

Nearly a thousand years ago, Zhou Dunyi wrote:

> The lotus is the gentleman among flowers.

The external forms of the world have changed, yet the problem of preserving one’s character within a complex environment remains.

Mud does not disappear.

Worldly splendour does not cease to attract the many.

The true difficulty is how a person may understand the world and still remain lucid, upright and inwardly uncorrupted.

May everyone who lives within the dust of the world preserve a clear lotus pond within the heart.
