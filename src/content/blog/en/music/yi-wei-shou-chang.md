Some songs are not written to mourn the passing of time.

They are written to preserve a posture: when the last light falls upon the shoulder, when frost and wind have carved their traces into the brow, when the road ahead becomes vast and uncertain, a person may still raise a cup toward the sky and say — this is not the end.

**The Will Remains** is a modern Chinese Mandopop R&B Rock song inspired by Cao Cao’s classical poem *Though the Tortoise Lives Long*. It begins with the heroic spirit of the late Eastern Han period and extends that spirit into a modern emotional landscape of northern old cities, river winds, fading sunlight, distant roads, and a single thought that has not yet grown cold.

This is not merely a musical adaptation of an ancient poem. It is an attempt to let Cao Cao’s vision of age, fate, ambition, and inner strength pass through history and return as a contemporary song about endurance, maturity, and the will to keep moving forward.

The song is not only about Cao Cao’s heroic ambition. It is about the light that remains inside every person who has already crossed through wind and frost, yet still refuses to step away from life’s unfinished stage.

### From “An Old Steed in the Stable” to “The Will Remains”

Cao Cao’s *Though the Tortoise Lives Long* has endured for centuries because it is not simply a poem of heroic pride. It is a poem written by someone who has seen clearly that life is finite — and yet still refuses to surrender his inner fire to age or fate.

> An old steed in the stable  
> still aspires to gallop a thousand miles.  
> A noble-hearted man in his later years  
> does not let his ambition fade.

The power of these lines lies not in denying time, but in acknowledging it. The divine tortoise, though long-lived, still has its end. The soaring serpent, though riding the mist, will return to dust.

Life has limits. Rise and decline have their seasons. Cao Cao sees this clearly. And precisely because he sees it clearly, the phrase “the noble heart does not cease” becomes all the more powerful.

*The Will Remains* begins from this point. It does not portray Cao Cao as a man who refuses to recognize age, defeat, or heaven’s decree. Instead, it imagines a person who has endured the rise and fall of fortune, who understands the weight of years, and who still preserves an inner resolve that has not been extinguished.

The pine has passed through snow, yet its green branches still stand upright.  
The wild swan crosses layers of mist, still flying toward the slanting glow.  
The frost-bright blade has returned to its sheath, yet the heart within has not gone still.  
White-haired, the figure lifts his sleeves, and his heroic spirit remains unchanged.

These images are not about youthful defiance. They are about a deeper strength — the strength that remains after time has already done its work.

This is the emotional foundation of *The Will Remains*: **Life may enter its fading light, but the will does not have to leave the stage.**

### Mandopop R&B Rock: Bringing the Spirit of Cao Cao into a Modern Pulse

Musically, *The Will Remains* is built on a mid-tempo Mandopop foundation, blending R&B, Hip Hop, Rock style C-pop, acoustic guitar, classic R&B beats, vintage vinyl crackle, distinct guzheng and erhu riffs, and a pentatonic melodic sensibility.

It is not a conventional “Three Kingdoms soundtrack,” nor is it a simple arrangement of Chinese instruments over a modern beat. It is closer to a contemporary Chinese storytelling song — one that carries, beneath its modern rhythm, the solemn power and emotional breadth of Cao Cao’s original poem.

* **Acoustic guitar** gives the song a feeling of journey and return, as if someone has walked a long road before finally beginning to speak.
* **Classic R&B beats** bring classical poetic emotion into the rhythm of the body, making “the noble heart does not cease” feel not only historical, but alive.
* **Guzheng and erhu riffs** act not as decorative markers of “Eastern style,” but as echoes from the winds of the late Han world.
* **Rock style C-pop energy** allows the restrained force of the verses to open into a powerful modern chorus.

In the chorus, the ancient spirit finally becomes a sentence that speaks directly to the present:

> 若餘暉落在肩上，我不把它當作散場。  
> *When the last light falls upon my shoulder, I will not take it as the closing scene.*

This is the song’s central transformation. The fading light is not failure. It is not merely an ending. It is the moment when life enters another kind of illumination. When a person no longer asks how long the years may be, the frost and wind upon the body can become the very thing that sharpens the brow.

The emotional core of the entire song arrives in the final lines:

> 風過高岡，燈影微茫。人向遠方，意未收場……  
> *The wind passes over the high ridge. The lights grow faint. The figure walks toward the distance. The will remains.*

The world grows quiet. The lights are no longer bright. Yet the person continues forward. This is not a loud declaration of victory. It is something more mature: there is nothing left to prove, nothing to demand back from time — only the quiet knowledge that something within has not ended.

### The MV: From the High Terraces of the Late Eastern Han to a Modern Northern City

The MV for *The Will Remains* is built around a visual dialogue between ancient and modern time.

The original poem section is set in the late Eastern Han period: northern terraces, ancient roads, military stables, city towers, courtyards, dusk, wind, and open plains form a restrained historical world. Cao Cao is not presented as a exaggerated battlefield hero, but as a man in his early fifties — a statesman, military commander, and poet whose gaze has already passed through history, ambition, and the knowledge of mortality.

The expanded poem and chorus move into a modern northern landscape: a return journey from the Shanhai Pass and Qinhuangdao region toward the Henan-Hebei borderlands, old cities, riverbanks, long embankments, bridge lights, night roads, and wind-blown high ground.

The modern male protagonist shares the same face as Cao Cao. This is not a simple reincarnation device, nor a fantasy of rebirth. It is a visual way of saying that classical poetic spirit does not remain sealed inside history. It can cross time and reappear in someone still walking through the present.

A thousand years ago, someone stood on a high terrace and wrote of a heart that would not cease. Today, someone stands by a river in the night wind and refuses to call the fading light an ending.

When the chorus reaches: **“只讓胸中熱浪，再一次漫過大江！”** (*Let the heat in this chest roll over the great river once more!*) the MV expands from the individual body into the open world. The river is not a visual effect. It is the scale of the feeling itself — the inner heat of a person who has passed through silence and begins, once again, to move.

### A Song for Those Still on the Road

Perhaps everyone carries an unfinished self within.

It may be a dream from youth that was never fully abandoned. It may be an ambition that remains after middle age. It may be the warmth that reality failed to erase. Or it may be a thought you believed had fallen silent, only to find it glowing again at dusk.

*The Will Remains* does not ask us to pretend that time has no power. It does not ask us to fight everything. It simply gives sound to a posture: after all the rise and fall, after all the wind and frost, one may still continue.

> 不問歲月短長，只讓胸中熱浪，再一次漫過大江！  
> *No matter how long or short the years, let the heat in this chest roll over the great river once more!*

This is not the recklessness of youth. It is the calm fire known only to those who have lived through time.
