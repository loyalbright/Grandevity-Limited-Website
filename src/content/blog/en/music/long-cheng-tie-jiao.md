In an era where streaming melodies move faster and faster, how can classical literature enter contemporary music again?

Not by simply placing guzheng over modern beats.  
Not by turning ancient poetry into empty spectacle.  
And not by forcing every classical work into a loud heroic narrative.

The deeper task is to let the loneliness, coldness, vigilance, and human tenderness hidden inside the poem breathe again through a new sound structure.

Today, Grandevity Studio officially releases a new conceptual single in its **Classical Literature Reimagined in Music** project:

**The Iron Horn of Longcheng**  
《龍城鐵角》

Inspired by Wang Changling’s celebrated Tang Dynasty frontier poem 《出塞》 (*Beyond the Pass*), this work is not a simple musical setting of an ancient poem. It is a full reconstruction across poetry, lyrics, arrangement, and cinematic visual language.

Our goal is to allow a thousand-year-old frontier lament to enter the soundscape of modern urban music.

### From Beyond the Pass: Not Bloodfire, but Watchfulness in the Cold

Wang Changling’s *Beyond the Pass* has endured for more than a thousand years not merely because it speaks of war, borderlands, and legendary generals.

Its true emotional force lies in its restraint.

> 秦時明月漢時關，萬里長征人未還。  
> 但使龍城飛將在，不教胡馬度陰山。

There is no direct weeping in the poem.  
No excessive battlefield description.  
No dramatic outcry.

Instead, Wang Changling places several images side by side: the moon of Qin, the passes of Han, a long expedition across ten thousand miles, and those who have not returned.

With only a few strokes, time, geography, history, and human fate all sink into a single cold silence.

For this reason, when creating *The Iron Horn of Longcheng*, we did not want to produce a conventional “heroic war anthem.” What moved us more was this image:

**When snow and wind close in, when the ruined city is silent, someone is still standing there.**

That quiet act of watchfulness is the emotional core of this song.

In the original expanded verse, we extend the frontier atmosphere through more cinematic imagery:

朔野寒雲覆舊巒，枯旗殘照隱孤川。<br>

There is no shouting of loyalty. No forced sentimentality. Only the northern wilderness, cold clouds, old mountains, withered banners, fading light, a lonely river, an iron horn cutting through the night, and a soldier’s robe reflecting snow-cold light.

The listener is invited to enter that border city in the storm.

### Music Design: Urban R&B Carrying Frontier Coldness

Musically, *The Iron Horn of Longcheng* avoids the common “grand Chinese epic” approach.

The drums do not thunder from the beginning.  
The strings do not pile up into cheap grandeur.  
The arrangement does not try to overwhelm the listener.

Instead, the song uses a more restrained and contemporary sound design: a mid-tempo urban R&B foundation, where classical frontier imagery and modern rhythm meet in the shadows.

*   **The guzheng** is not decoration. It appears like a cold string of light flashing briefly through the dark.
*   **The low end** is not for display. It feels like wind and snow pressing in from the distance.
*   **The vocal** is not a battle cry. It approaches the edge of emotion through silence and control.

The chorus marks the true emotional turn of the song:

> 聽朔風，一夜吹白舊邊土；  
> 看孤燈，還亮在荒城深處。  
> 我把此身，留給天明前的長路；  
> 若有人，問我為何踏上不歸途；  
> **只因身後，還有萬家燈火如初。**

The line “萬家燈火如初” — *“the lights of countless homes still remain as they were”* — is the emotional anchor of the entire song.

Before this moment, the world is cold: wind, snow, ruined city, lonely lamp, endless road. But when this line arrives, the listener finally sees the human world behind the frontier.

The loneliness of the border is no longer only one person’s fate. It becomes a deeper form of protection. Someone does not return because behind him, countless homes must still remain warm and lit.

### Why “The Iron Horn of Longcheng”?

The Chinese title **龍城鐵角** combines two powerful images.

*   **Longcheng** refers to the legendary frontier space associated with military defense and the heroic imagination of the Tang frontier tradition. It evokes not only a place, but an idea: the final line between home and the unknown.
*   **Iron horn** suggests a military signal cutting through the cold night. It is not a decorative sound. It is a call, a warning, a memory, and a sign that someone is still awake when the rest of the world is sleeping.

Together, *The Iron Horn of Longcheng* is not simply a title about war. It is a title about vigilance. About one sound rising in the snow. About one person standing between danger and home. About the reason a lonely lamp still burns in a ruined city.

### “The Lights of Countless Homes Still Remain”

The emotional center of the song is this line:

> **只因身後，還有萬家燈火如初。**  
> *Because behind me, the lights of countless homes still remain as before.*

This line turns the song away from the individual and back toward the human world.

Before it, the images are almost entirely cold: northern wind, snow, border soil, ruined city, lonely lamp, and the road before dawn. But then, suddenly, there is warmth.

Not a large sentimental statement.  
Not a heroic slogan.  
Only lights.

The lights of homes. The lights of families. The lights of ordinary lives that continue because someone else is willing to remain outside the warmth. 

This is why the song does not need to shout. Its power lies in the contrast between one cold border and countless warm windows.

### About the Creative Structure

The lyric structure does not force the ancient poem into a plot summary. Instead, it moves in three stages:

1.  First, it quotes Wang Changling’s original poem, preserving the historical depth and frontier grandeur of the Tang verse.
2.  Second, it expands the frontier landscape through original cinematic imagery: cold clouds, withered banners, lonely rivers, iron horns, and snow-lit battle robes.
3.  Third, it lands in a modern emotional core that contemporary listeners can immediately understand:

To guard the frontier is not to become a hero. **It is to keep the world behind you safe enough to remain ordinary.** That is the deepest emotional movement of *The Iron Horn of Longcheng*.
