Some forms of love are not meant to arrive.

They simply lead someone along the water for a very long time—through white dew, reeds, morning mist and an old bridge—until one truth becomes clear: some people never reach our side, yet never leave our heart.

**Far Away, Yet in My Heart** is a Mandarin male-vocal R&B and Chinese-inspired song based on **“Jian Jia”**, a poem from the *Airs of Qin* in *The Book of Songs*.

The song preserves the complete classical poem before opening a modern continuation with the line:

> Green willows line the shore; dawn mist carries fragrance.

The reeds, white dew, distant beloved and unending pursuit of the ancient Zhou wetland travel across more than two thousand years, reappearing as willows, morning mist, a stone bridge, a flower-lined path and a quiet figure beside modern West Lake in Hangzhou.

This is not simply an ancient poem set to music, nor does it attempt to give the original poem a convenient happy ending.

Its central question is more difficult:

**When distance remains, can longing still find where it belongs?**

### From “The Reeds Are Lush” to “Green Willows Line the Shore”

“Jian Jia” has remained deeply moving for centuries not only because “the one I call my beloved stands beyond the water” became one of the most enduring images of longing in Chinese literature, but also because the poem never tells us exactly who the distant figure is.

> The reeds are lush and green; white dew turns to frost.  
> The one I call my beloved stands somewhere beyond the water.

The speaker follows the river again and again.

He travels upstream, but the way is difficult and long. He follows the current, only to see the beloved apparently standing in the middle of the water. Every attempt to draw closer makes the distance more visible.

The water is therefore more than a physical barrier. It becomes the boundary between desire and arrival, recognition and possession, presence and absence.

The modern continuation of **Far Away, Yet in My Heart** does not remove that distance. Instead, it answers the ancient poem through a new landscape:

<div style="background: rgba(255, 255, 255, 0.03); border: 1px solid rgba(255, 255, 255, 0.05); padding: 2rem; border-radius: 12px; margin: 2rem 0; font-size: 1.05rem; line-height: 2;">
Green willows line the shore; dawn mist carries fragrance.<br>
The face I hold in mind stands far beside the bridge.<br>
Following the embankment, I follow her shadow; the flowered path stretches on.<br>
Following the ripples, I follow her reflection, as if it moved upon the clear waves.
</div>

The original poem is built through recurring images: reeds, white dew, the distant beloved, the upstream pursuit and the downstream pursuit.

The new poem responds with its own parallel structure: shore willows, dawn mist, the remembered face, following the shadow along the embankment and following the reflection across the water.

Reeds become willows. White dew becomes morning mist. The beloved in the water becomes a quiet figure beside a bridge.

The era changes, but the emotional movement remains the same.

What survives is not one specific landscape, but the restraint of someone who longs to draw near and yet hesitates to disturb what is still beautiful at a distance.

### Modern R&B: Turning Repetition into a Heartbeat

Musically, **Far Away, Yet in My Heart** combines mid-tempo Mandopop, R&B, hip hop and rock.

Acoustic guitar, classic R&B drums, deep bass and subtle vinyl crackle form the modern narrative foundation. Guzheng and erhu appear like fragments of melody emerging through mist, connecting the ancient and contemporary worlds.

The traditional instruments are not placed over a pop arrangement merely to signal “Chinese style.”

* The **acoustic guitar** creates an intimate, close-range storytelling voice.
* The **R&B rhythm** transforms the repeating stanzas of “Jian Jia” into a modern physical pulse.
* The **guzheng and erhu** act as musical traces through which the two eras recognise one another.
* The controlled **rock lift** in the chorus releases the accumulated emotion without turning the song into shouting or melodrama.

The song’s central emotional turn arrives here:

> I followed your shadow here,  
> Only then knowing even one journey can be so long.  
> You never reach my side,  
> **Yet you remain far within my heart.**

“You never reach my side” acknowledges the distance of reality.

“Yet you remain far within my heart” transforms that distance. The beloved may never truly arrive, but has already become impossible to remove from the inner world.

### One Silent Glance, Ten Thousand Hidden Words

The song does not move forward through a dramatic declaration.

Its emotion remains in a backward glance, an unfinished step, a held gaze and the words that never become sound:

> I long to draw near, then stop.  
> A thousand words fade into the haze.  
> Deep within the silence,  
> Even the smallest thought knows where it belongs.

The moment of stopping is not indecision. It is a form of care.

Deep emotion does not always rush toward possession. Sometimes remaining a few steps away preserves the other person more completely than an embrace ever could.

The bridge section brings this restraint to its quietest point:

> The mist passes the shore.  
> The willows continue to grow.  
> One glance, without a word;  
> Ten thousand words remain concealed.

The mist will pass. The willows will continue to grow.

The words are never spoken, but the silence is not empty. Everything that failed to arrive has already been held within that single look.

### The MV: From an Ancient Zhou Wetland to Modern West Lake

The music video unfolds across two connected eras.

The classical section is set in a southern wetland during the Western Zhou to early Spring and Autumn period: frost-covered reeds, winding waterways, earthen embankments, primitive timber footbridges and distant timber-and-thatch settlements create the world of “Jian Jia.”

The modern section moves to West Lake in Hangzhou on a deep-autumn morning: willow-lined banks, dawn mist, flower paths, stone steps, a single-arch bridge, white walls, dark roof tiles and a restrained modern skyline.

The ancient and modern couples share the same faces and body proportions.

This is not intended as a simple reincarnation romance. It is a visual expression of emotional continuity: a gaze that could not arrive thousands of years ago still recognises its counterpart in the present.

The colour palette also evolves with the story.

The Zhou wetland is built from blue-grey mist, frost-white reeds and cold dawn light. When the story enters modern Hangzhou, the morning gradually turns towards soft gold.

At the end, the two characters face each other across the bridge. The woman smiles. The man’s expression softens. They still do not embrace.

The song does not require physical reunion in order to be complete.

Its true resolution is the knowledge that the unspoken feeling has never lost its direction.

### A Song for Those We Can See but May Never Reach

Perhaps everyone carries someone who remains “beyond the water.”

That person may once have stood before us. Or they may exist only in a memory, a particular morning, an old bridge or a final glance that ended before the words were spoken.

**Far Away, Yet in My Heart** does not attempt to decide how such a relationship should end.

It simply recognises that a feeling does not become unreal merely because it never reaches physical closeness.

> You never reach my side, yet you remain far within my heart.

It is neither possession nor forgetting.

It is the decision to preserve a clear place for someone across the long movement of time.

### About the Project

**Far Away, Yet in My Heart** is part of Grandevity Studio’s *Classical Literature Reimagined in Music* series.

The project seeks to bring classical Chinese literature beyond textbooks, annotations and memorisation, allowing it to re-enter contemporary rhythm, sound, imagery and emotional life.

More than two thousand years ago, someone followed the water in search of a figure standing beyond it.

More than two thousand years later, someone turns beside a bridge at West Lake and finally understands:

Some people never reach our side, yet remain forever within our heart.
