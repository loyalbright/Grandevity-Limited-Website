In this fast, restless, and noisy age, we still believe one thing:

Words that have survived for a thousand years are not museum specimens. They are still capable of becoming echoes inside the modern heart.

Nearly a millennium ago, Su Shi stood at Red Cliff after being exiled to Huangzhou. Facing the great river, jagged rocks, crashing waves, and the memory of ancient heroes, he wrote 《念奴嬌・赤壁懷古》 (*Nian Nu Jiao: Remembrance at Red Cliff*) — one of the greatest lyric poems in Chinese literature.

Among its most unforgettable lines is this:

> 多情應笑我，早生華髮。  
> *My sentimentality should laugh at me — for my hair has turned gray too soon.*

It is a line about more than nostalgia. It carries the ache of heroic ages already gone, and the clarity of a person confronting time, ambition, fate, and the self.

Nearly a thousand years later, we stand under neon lights in modern cities. We look at missed dreams, unreachable hands, unfinished roads, and lives that still ask where they are going.

The world has changed. Rivers and mountains have become towers and streets. War fire has become city light. But the loneliness, loss, and final release that human beings feel before the vastness of time have never truly disappeared.

Today, Grandevity Studio officially releases a major work in its **Classical Literature Reimagined in Music** project:

**Jiangyue Yu Nihong — River Moon and Neon**  
《江月與霓虹》

This is not simply a Chinese-style song. It is a new meeting between Su Shi, Red Cliff, the river moon, and the modern city.

### When Song Dynasty Ci Meets R&B

In creating *Jiangyue Yu Nihong*, we did not want to simply place classical lines into a modern melody. Nor did we want to turn “Chinese style” into decorative language or a pile of elegant words.

The song opens by quoting Su Shi’s original 《念奴嬌・赤壁懷古》 in full. Acoustic guitar, guzheng textures, R&B drums, and deep bass unfold together, allowing Red Cliff to emerge not as a textbook memory, but as a slowly expanding sonic landscape.

The second verse is an original expansion built upon the tonal weight and emotional architecture of the original *ci* form. It moves from:

The language continues the solemn force of *Nian Nu Jiao*, while shifting the viewpoint from the age of heroes to a later traveler looking back alone. This is not a repetition of the original poem. It is an after-echo of Su Shi’s spirit in another age.

Then, when the transition line arrives —

> 清輝未冷，照入今宵燈火。  
> *The clear moonlight has not yet grown cold; it shines into tonight’s city lights.*

— the ancient river moon slowly enters the modern city. The chorus no longer remains in historical reflection. It steps fully into today’s streets:

> 霓虹閃爍的街頭，誰在深夜裡停留。  
> 錯過的夢，握不住的手，最終淹沒在洪流。

This is the emotional core of *Jiangyue Yu Nihong*: The moon of the ancients has not vanished. It has only changed the way it shines upon us.

### Why “River Moon and Neon”?

The Chinese title **江月與霓虹** brings together two worlds.

**江月 (River Moon)** belongs to Su Shi, Red Cliff, old history, heroic memory, and the eternal light that shines over time.  
**霓虹 (Neon)** belongs to the modern city: late-night streets, high-rises, traffic, loneliness, missed dreams, and the restless rhythm of contemporary life.

At first, these two images seem far apart. One is ancient. One is modern. One belongs to the river. One belongs to the city. But the song suggests that they illuminate the same human condition.

The river moon once shone upon exiled poets, vanished heroes, and those who looked back at history with wine in hand. Neon now shines upon modern people who walk through the night, carrying dreams they could not keep and choices they cannot fully explain.

Different lights. The same solitude.

### “The Clear Moonlight Has Not Yet Grown Cold”

The emotional hinge of the song is this line:

> **清輝未冷，照入今宵燈火。**  
> *The clear moonlight has not yet grown cold; it shines into tonight’s city lights.*

This line allows the whole song to cross from ancient history into modern life.

Before it, the listener is standing at Red Cliff — among waves, rocks, ruined ramparts, and memories of heroes. After it, the listener enters the city — among neon lights, high-rise buildings, late-night streets, and the silent freedom of letting go.

The moonlight has not gone cold because classical literature has not lost its warmth. It still reaches us. Even through glass towers. Even through traffic. Even through neon.

### About the Creative Structure

The structure of *Jiangyue Yu Nihong* moves through three major layers.

1.  First, it preserves Su Shi’s original *Nian Nu Jiao*, allowing the ancient poem to stand with full dignity and force.
2.  Second, it expands the classical landscape through a new original verse, written in the shadow of the original *ci* form. This verse continues the mood of Red Cliff, but shifts from the heroic past to the lonely gaze of a later traveler.
3.  Third, the song breaks into a modern R&B chorus, where the emotional landscape becomes contemporary: neon streets, missed dreams, high-rise buildings, strong liquor, and silent freedom.

This is not a simple adaptation. It is a time-space crossing. The past does not remain behind us. It returns as an echo in the modern night.
