Some large houses cannot contain a peaceful heart.

Some small rooms can hold mountains, water, friendship, books, music and the complete dignity of a human life.

**The Humble Room Inscription** is a Mandarin male-vocal R&B work based on Liu Yuxi’s celebrated Tang-dynasty text **Lou Shi Ming**.

The song does not add a modern storyline or invent new narrative episodes. Its verses, pre-chorus and principal choruses retain Liu Yuxi’s original text. The bridge condenses and rhythmically reorganises several original lines so that the classical imagery can function naturally within a contemporary song structure.

Musically, the work combines melancholic Mandopop R&B, a dynamic 1990s urban groove, acoustic guitar, guzheng motifs, sweeping strings, vinyl warmth, layered harmonies and an extended erhu solo.

A short classical inscription becomes a modern song about dignity, intellectual freedom, friendship and the right to preserve an inner order when the external world becomes narrow.

> This is a humble room, yet fragrant with virtue.

**Lou Shi Ming** does not deny the importance of material life, nor does it romanticise hardship.

Instead, it raises a more fundamental question:

**If external circumstances cannot determine a person’s true worth, by what measure should a life be judged?**

Liu Yuxi’s answer is not the size of a house, an official title, social rank or wealth.

It is integrity, learning, companionship, music, reading and the freedom not to be ruled by noise.

### A Mountain Need Not Be High: Value Beyond External Scale

The inscription does not begin with the room itself.

It begins with mountains and water:

> A mountain need not be high; if an immortal dwells there, it becomes renowned.  
> Water need not be deep; if a dragon inhabits it, it becomes numinous.

The reputation of a mountain does not depend entirely upon its height.

The spiritual vitality of water does not depend entirely upon its depth.

What transforms them is the presence of the immortal and the dragon.

Through this concise analogy, Liu Yuxi establishes a system of value different from the one ordinarily imposed by society.

Height does not guarantee nobility.

Depth does not guarantee wonder.

In the same way, the worth of a dwelling does not depend upon its size, decoration, price or prestige.

Only after dismantling these external measures does Liu Yuxi introduce the humble room.

The music opens without oversized percussion. Restrained male vocals, acoustic guitar and subtle guzheng preserve the short, balanced movement of the original phrases.

In the MV, the mountain is modest and the stream is shallow. Yet bamboo shadows, reflected light and a quiet courtyard give ordinary scenery an unmistakable inward radiance.

The visual meaning is clear:

**What is truly valuable does not always appear in an impressive form.**

### “This Is a Humble Room”: Accepting Simplicity Without Accepting Inferiority

Liu Yuxi then writes:

> This is a humble room, yet fragrant with virtue.

The first half of the sentence does not deny reality.

The room is modest. It has no monumental hall, elaborate furnishing or prestigious entrance.

The second half immediately changes the direction of the thought.

The Chinese word *xin* originally refers to a fragrance spreading outward. Here it suggests moral excellence and an honourable reputation.

The room ceases to be humble not because the author pretends it is luxurious, but because the person who lives there refuses to let external limitation reduce his inner world.

This is not merely optimistic self-consolation.

It is a lucid judgement:

**I recognise the limits of my circumstances, but I refuse to allow those limits to define me.**

At this line, the song begins to open. Layered harmonies and strings lift the word “virtue,” turning the philosophical shift into an audible emotional expansion.

The MV retains old timber, bamboo blinds, moss-covered steps, a plain qin and scrolls.

The residence is neither depicted as miserable ruin nor transformed into an extravagant fantasy garden. It remains simple, clean, settled and intellectually refined.

### Moss Climbs the Steps: Nature Entering Daily Life

Liu Yuxi continues:

> Green traces of moss climb the steps; the colour of grass enters the curtain.

There are no rare flowers and no highly designed garden.

There is only moss and grass.

Moss grows upward along damp stone steps. The green of the plants enters the room through the bamboo curtain.

The verbs “climb” and “enter” give direction and movement to the natural world.

Nature is not kept outside as something merely to be viewed. It crosses the steps, threshold and curtain, becoming part of ordinary life.

The humble room therefore becomes more than a small building.

It is connected to stone, grass, bamboo, water and season, forming a world that remains open and capable of breathing.

The MV is set in late spring and early summer.

Moss is wet after rain. Drops remain on the bamboo leaves. Morning light passes through the curtain, slowly moving across the walls and desk.

The scholar’s wife gently lifts the bamboo screen while his silhouette remains deeper inside the room.

These details do not create richness through accumulation.

They reveal another form of abundance:

**The room is rich because it remains fully connected to the living world around it.**

### Learned Friends in Conversation: Wealth as Human Connection

The inscription continues:

> In conversation and laughter are learned scholars; among those who come and go, none are unlettered.

The phrase *hongru* refers to highly learned scholars.

The historical term *baiding* refers to people without official rank or literary education and belongs to the social vocabulary of its period.

The deeper emphasis of these lines lies not simply in social distinction, but in the form of companionship Liu Yuxi values.

Although the room is small, people still come to discuss learning, exchange ideas, drink tea and speak freely.

The room’s worth is not determined by carriages, ceremony or display.

It is determined by the quality of what occurs within it.

A low tea table, a few scrolls and several people capable of genuinely understanding one another are enough to create an entire intellectual community.

Here the song introduces a clearer 1990s Mandopop R&B pulse.

The drums and bass move forward, and the vocal changes from private reflection into a warmer social register.

In the MV, scholars gather in the bamboo courtyard around tea and open scrolls.

The wife and young maid serve tea in the background. Their presence brings warmth and makes the humble room more than an isolated symbol of individual pride.

It becomes a lived human world containing conversation, laughter and companionship.

### Tuning the Plain Qin and Reading the Scriptures

> Here I may tune my plain qin and read the Golden Scriptures.

The “plain qin” is an undecorated instrument, suggesting simplicity and purity rather than performance or display.

The “Golden Scriptures” are commonly understood as Buddhist texts.

One instrument and one text bring together music and language, emotional cultivation and spiritual thought.

The phrase “I may” is especially important.

Liu Yuxi is not boasting about rare possessions.

He is describing the freedom that remains available to him within the humble room.

He may play music.

He may read.

He may think.

He may return his time to himself.

This freedom is quiet and may remain invisible to the outside world.

Yet it forms the true centre of the room.

The arrangement combines acoustic guitar, guzheng and strings.

The guzheng does not function merely as a decorative sign of tradition. It becomes an echo of the plain qin within a modern R&B harmonic space.

In the MV, the scholar tunes the instrument at a low desk while the scriptures lie beside him. His wife listens quietly near the bamboo curtain.

It is not a stage performance.

It is the moment in which a person becomes whole again within the order of his own life.

### No Clamour to Disturb the Ear, No Documents to Weary the Body

> There is no clamour of strings and flutes to disturb the ear, and no official documents to weary the body.

“Strings and flutes” literally names musical instruments, but here it points toward noisy entertainment, social display and the distractions of worldly gatherings.

“Official documents” refers to bureaucratic paperwork.

One invades the senses.

The other exhausts the body.

On the surface, these lines describe what the room lacks.

At a deeper level, they describe the space the author has deliberately preserved.

No endless social obligation.

No administrative burden consuming every hour.

No constant stream of external demands occupying attention.

True quiet does not require the world to become completely silent.

It requires the ability to decide which sounds are permitted to enter one’s life.

The music creates contrast through stronger rhythm and strings. The outer world appears to approach, while the interior remains centred.

In the MV, documents are lifted by the wind and the distant silhouettes of musicians and official messengers pass beyond the room.

Inside remain the qin, the scriptures, the lamp and bamboo shadows.

The scholar neither protests dramatically nor responds with anger.

He simply remains composed.

That capacity to preserve an undisturbed centre is among the most modern—and most difficult—qualities of Liu Yuxi’s text.

### Zhuge Liang’s Cottage and Yang Xiong’s Pavilion

The inscription concludes:

> The cottage of Zhuge Liang in Nanyang; the pavilion of Yang Xiong in Western Shu.  
> Confucius said: “What humbleness could there be?”

The Nanyang cottage refers to Zhuge Liang’s dwelling before entering public service.

The Western Shu pavilion refers to the residence of the Han-dynasty writer Yang Xiong, also known as Ziyun.

These allusions connect Liu Yuxi’s room to the dwellings of earlier learned and principled figures.

A thatched cottage and a small pavilion were not impressive forms of architecture.

Yet because of the people who lived within them, they became part of enduring cultural memory.

Liu Yuxi does not directly proclaim that his room is equal to theirs.

He places the three dwellings beside one another and allows the reader to recognise the spiritual relationship.

The final question reverses the ordinary sentence order of classical Chinese and literally means: “What humbleness is there?”

It is not a request for reassurance.

It is a rhetorical question spoken after the judgement has already been completed:

**If integrity, learning and inner freedom are present, what can truly be called humble?**

The song pauses before its final line.

“Confucius said” becomes a threshold, suspending the melody.

“What humbleness could there be?” then arrives in a higher register as the philosophical conclusion of the work.

### The Adapted Bridge: Condensing Rather Than Expanding the Original

The bridge reads:

> Moss green, grass blue;  
> Learned friends in conversation, none unlettered.  
> Tune the plain qin, read the scriptures;  
> No clamour, and official burdens become light.

These lines are not a word-for-word reproduction of Liu Yuxi’s text.

They condense the following original phrases:

> Green traces of moss climb the steps; the colour of grass enters the curtain.  
> In conversation and laughter are learned scholars; among those who come and go, none are unlettered.  
> Here I may tune my plain qin and read the Golden Scriptures.  
> There is no clamour of strings and flutes to disturb the ear, and no official documents to weary the body.

The bridge does not introduce new modern characters, narrative events or a separate storyline.

It removes grammatical particles, compresses phrases and reorganises rhythm, transforming the original imagery into short lines suitable for the musical build before the beat drop.

The most accurate description of the lyrics is therefore:

**The principal lyrics use Liu Yuxi’s original text, without modern narrative expansion; the bridge contains condensation and lyrical adaptation.**

Crediting **Lyrics Adaptation** acknowledges the structural work of the modern song while preserving Liu Yuxi’s authorship of the classical source.

### Melancholic Mandopop R&B: Classical Chinese Beyond Recitation

The original **Lou Shi Ming** is brief, balanced and highly rhythmic. Its parallel phrases and recurring sounds already contain strong musical potential.

This production does not use traditional recitation, operatic vocal style or oversized historical spectacle.

Instead, it is built upon melancholic Mandopop R&B and a dynamic 1990s urban groove.

The arrangement combines:

* **A 1990s urban pulse**, giving the classical phrases a natural contemporary movement.
* **Warm, restrained Mandarin male vocals**, avoiding shouting and exaggerated theatrical delivery.
* **Acoustic guitar**, creating a clear, nostalgic and human rhythmic foundation.
* **Guzheng motifs**, answering the bamboo, grass, water and qin imagery.
* **Sweeping strings**, expanding the emotional space of the choruses and conclusion.
* **Vinyl texture**, bringing the warmth of a 1990s Mandarin record.
* **Layered harmonies and falsetto runs**, extending the emotional life of the concise classical phrases.
* **An erhu solo**, carrying the listener beyond the room into moonlight, bamboo and a wider inner landscape.

The purpose is not a superficial mixture of ancient costume and contemporary pop.

It is to allow the rhythm, values and emotional intelligence of the classical text to become convincing within the language of modern Mandarin music.

### The Erhu Solo: From a Small Room into the Boundless Night

The central erhu solo lasts approximately twenty-four seconds.

When the words stop, the music no longer explains the humble room.

It allows the audience to enter its night directly.

The MV centres on a female musician wearing a pale blue-green Tang-style dress.

She performs a bowed huqin-type instrument beside bamboo, mossy stones and a shallow stream. Lantern light falls across the instrument and her sleeves. Blue night is reflected in the water, while the scholar watches quietly from the doorway or deeper within the courtyard.

The sequence moves through wide shots, side profiles, intimate portraits and details of the bow and strings.

The bow moves.

Bamboo shadows sway.

Reflections tremble.

The wife, book boy and visiting scholars stand quietly at the entrance.

Although the room is physically small, the music connects it to the entire bamboo grove, stream and moonlit sky.

The erhu solo completes the most important spatial transformation in the song:

**From living in a humble room to possessing an inward world without boundaries.**

### The MV: A Scholar Who Refuses to Be Defined by Circumstance

The entire MV remains within an imagined Middle Tang setting.

There is no modern timeline and no transition between past and present.

The visual journey begins in the mist of late spring and early summer, moving through warm afternoon light into the blue tones of night.

The principal character is a Tang scholar of approximately forty years of age. He is tall and slender, with a restrained beard, wearing a dark blue-grey round-collar robe, an ivory undergarment and a black futou headpiece.

He is not portrayed as an official loudly protesting his fate.

His emotional language remains controlled:

He quietly accepts the humble residence.

He retains tenderness toward his wife.

He relaxes among learned friends.

He returns to order through music and reading.

When he walks into the bamboo courtyard at night, he finally appears free from the need to answer the outer world.

His wife wears pale lotus-pink and moon-white robes. A young maid wears pale blue. The book boy grinds ink, arranges scrolls and receives visitors.

These figures do not create palace-like splendour.

They make the humble room feel inhabited, human and emotionally credible.

The opening sequence moves through landscape, the residence, the scholar’s arrival, his family’s welcome and the book boy preparing ink.

The lyrical scenes then follow the progression of the text:

mountains and water, the interior of the room, moss and grass, learned friends, qin and scripture, freedom from documents, the dwellings of ancient sages and the final question.

The outro returns to fading lamplight, the moonlit house and the scholar’s back facing bamboo and water.

He does not depart.

The camera simply withdraws until bamboo shadows, the stream and the warm light of the room become the final subjects.

### Not Merely a Text About a Room

**The Humble Room Inscription** has endured for more than a thousand years not only because of its brevity, balance and memorable parallel phrasing.

Its deeper power lies in a system of values that has not become obsolete.

The world often measures a person through property, price, position, income and visible achievement.

Liu Yuxi turns the measure inward:

Are there people worth speaking with?

Is there time to read and think in peace?

Can life remain free from endless documents and noise?

When circumstances become limited, can the heart refuse to become limited with them?

The humble room is not a romanticisation of poverty.

It is a spiritual proposition.

If the outer world removes prestige, comfort and applause, can a person still retain judgement, integrity, friendship and inward order?

The mountain need not be high.

The water need not be deep.

The room need not be magnificent.

What gives a place its radiance is ultimately the person who lives within it.

The final question—“What humbleness could there be?”—is therefore an answer not only about architecture, but about life itself.

### About the Project

**The Humble Room Inscription** is part of Grandevity Studio’s *Classical Literature Reimagined in Music* series.

The project seeks to bring classical Chinese literature beyond textbooks, annotations, recitation and memorisation, allowing it to re-enter contemporary melody, sound, imagery and emotional life.

Although **Lou Shi Ming** is often studied alongside famous Tang poems, its literary form is an inscription, or *ming*, rather than a poem.

It is brief and rhythmically ordered, combining mountain and water, moss and grass, music and books, friendship and philosophical judgement.

More than a thousand years ago, Liu Yuxi wrote:

> This is a humble room, yet fragrant with virtue.

More than a thousand years later, people may still find themselves living within different forms of “humble rooms”:

A stage of work in which they are not understood.

Financial circumstances that remain limited.

A life that appears temporarily stalled.

A talent that has not yet been recognised.

External space may be restricted.

Yet if a person retains integrity, friendship, reading, music and lucid judgement, the inner world need not become narrow.

A room is ultimately only a room.

What determines whether it is truly humble is the person who lives within it.
