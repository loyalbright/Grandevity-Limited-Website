In a noisy age, do we still have the patience to listen to a rainstorm from a thousand years ago?

That rain once fell on a mountain road in Huangzhou during the Northern Song Dynasty. It passed through bamboo forests, struck leaves, soaked straw sandals and old garments, and revealed something deep within the Chinese literary spirit: the ability to walk through wind and rain without surrendering the self.

For Grandevity Studio, classical poetry is not merely ink preserved on paper. It is a spiritual coordinate left by those who endured exile, loss, uncertainty, separation, and worldly change before us.

Today, we officially release a new original classical literature reimagined work:

**Yisuo Jiangyu**  
《一蓑江雨》

Inspired by Su Shi’s classic lyric poem 《定風波·莫聽穿林打葉聲》 (*Dingfengbo*), this song follows the poet’s spirit beyond the mountain road and into a moonlit river night — where rain, river, moonlight, solitude, and silence become a way of settling the modern heart.

---

### From Dingfengbo to the State of “Letting Life Be”

The inspiration for *Yisuo Jiangyu* comes from Su Shi’s famous poem *Dingfengbo*.

> 莫聽穿林打葉聲，何妨吟嘯且徐行。  
> *Do not listen to the rain beating through the forest leaves; why not chant and walk slowly?*

More than nine hundred years ago, on a muddy spring road in Huangzhou, Su Shi wrote one of the most graceful and resilient responses to adversity in Chinese literature.

But *Yisuo Jiangyu* does not simply reproduce the original poem. Instead, it continues along Su Shi’s inner road.

After the rain on the mountain path begins to fade, after the slanting sunlight appears, after twilight closes in — where does a person go within themselves? In this song, the space slowly shifts from “a mountain road in wind and rain” to “a cold river under moonlight.” The emotion moves from the boldness of “Who is afraid?” toward the clearer stillness of “letting floating life be.”

There is no forced declaration of strength. No theatrical heroism. No need to prove anything.

Only rain, river, moon, lone boat, distant islet, night air, and a fisherman’s song remain.

When rain soaks the old clothes, when a lone boat rests by the distant shore, when a person looks back across the vast human world from the heart of the river, all attachment, sorrow, resistance, and pride begin to dissolve into the night.

This is not only a modern extension of Song Dynasty aesthetics. It is a musical practice of using ancient spiritual strength to bring rest to contemporary life.

### Sound Design: After the Rain, the River Opens

In arrangement and production, *Yisuo Jiangyu* deliberately avoids the common excesses of conventional Chinese-style music. There is no overwhelming orchestral build-up. No forced emotional climax. No heavy drums trying to manufacture grandeur.

Instead, the sound design is restrained and spacious.

*   An acoustic guitar traces the simplicity of walking slowly along a mountain path.
*   A deep cello carries the weight of a weathered human world.
*   A distant xiao flute opens the moonlight over the river.

Everything in the arrangement steps back, allowing the words, the rain, and the surface of the river to emerge slowly. The melody moves like a person walking through rain. The arrangement expands like a river becoming still beneath moonlight.

True freedom is not the absence of storms. It is the ability to say, after the storm has passed:

> 也無風雨也無晴。  
> *There is no wind or rain. There is no clear sky either.*

### Why “A Straw Cloak in River Rain”?

The Chinese title **一蓑江雨** grows from Su Shi’s famous line:

> 一蓑煙雨任平生。  
> *In a straw cloak of mist and rain, I let life unfold.*

In the new song, the image moves from 煙雨 — mist and rain — to 江雨 — river rain. This small shift changes the emotional space. The original poem begins on a road. The song expands toward a river.

The straw cloak is no longer only protection from rain. It becomes a symbol of a person’s way of moving through life: lightly, slowly, without panic, without complaint.

To wear “a straw cloak in river rain” is to accept that storms will come, and that one can still remain whole within them.

### “There Is No Wind or Rain. There Is No Clear Sky Either.”

The emotional center of the song remains Su Shi’s great ending:

> 也無風雨也無晴。  
> *There is no wind or rain. There is no clear sky either.*

This line is often misunderstood as simple optimism. It is deeper than that. Su Shi does not say that hardship disappears. He does not insist that everything becomes bright. Instead, he reaches a state where the difference between storm and sunshine no longer rules the heart.

This is not denial. It is freedom.

The modern expanded chorus echoes this spirit:

> 一蓑江雨任浮生。  
> *In a straw cloak of river rain, I let this floating life be.*

For modern listeners, this may be the most necessary part of the song. Life may still be uncertain. The rain may still fall. But the heart no longer has to be dragged by every change in the weather.

### About the Creative Structure

The lyric structure of *Yisuo Jiangyu* follows a gradual inner movement.

1.  First, it preserves Su Shi’s original *Dingfengbo*, keeping the poem’s foundational spirit of calm resilience.
2.  Second, it extends the scene into a new poetic space: floating clouds, clear ripples, a lone boat, a distant islet, fine rain, misty dusk, and a vast cold river under the moon.
3.  Third, it allows the modern chorus to translate this ancient composure into a contemporary emotional language:

Let the rain fall. Let the river flow. Let the clouds cross the evening sky. Let life be.

This is not a song about escaping the world. **It is a song about no longer being broken by the world.**
